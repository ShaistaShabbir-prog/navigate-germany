🏦 Opening a German bank account

A German bank account (IBAN) is essential for receiving salary, paying rent, and setting up utilities. Open one within the first 2 weeks of arriving.
You need your Meldebescheinigung (Anmeldung confirmation) to open most German bank accounts.

📋 Steuer-ID & tax basics

Steuer-ID (Tax ID)

Your permanent 11-digit tax identification number. Sent automatically by post 2–4 weeks after Anmeldung.

  • Give it to your employer on day one
  • Required to receive salary correctly
  • Never changes, even if you leave Germany
  • Lost it? Request at bzst.de

Steuernummer (Tax number)

Assigned by your local Finanzamt. Used for filing tax returns.

  • Different from Steuer-ID
  • Changes if you move to another Finanzamt area
  • Needed for Elster (online tax filing)
  • Self-employed need this from day one

Tax classes (Steuerklassen)

Class I

Single, no children. Most immigrants start here.

Class II

Single parent. Reduced tax.

Class III

Higher earner in married couple. Lower tax.

Class IV

Both spouses earn similarly. Standard.

Class V

Lower earner in married couple. Higher tax.

Class VI

Second job. Highest tax rate.

File a tax return (Steuererklärung) — most employees in Class I get money back. Deadline: 31 July of the following year. Use elster.de (free) or Wundertax/Taxfix apps.

💡 Money tips for newcomers

  • Open a bank account in week 1 — you need IBAN for salary and rent
  • Keep your Meldebescheinigung — banks and employers will ask for it
  • File a tax return every year — most employees get money back
  • Build Schufa from day one — pay bills on time, get a credit card
  • Avoid cash advances on credit cards — fees are very high in Germany
  • Set up SEPA direct debits (Lastschrift) for rent and utilities
  • Keep receipts for work-related expenses — tax deductible
  • Use Wise for international transfers — never use your German bank for this
  • Check your Schufa score at meineschufa.de (free once per year)
